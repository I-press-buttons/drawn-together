# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Dates, friends, or small groups looking for a lighthearted icebreaker. They're in a social setting — at a bar, on a couch, at a dinner party — and want something that sparks conversation and laughter without feeling like a quiz or a test. They pick it up in seconds, play a round, and put it down. The game serves the social moment, not the other way around.

## Product Purpose

A date-matching game where players pair related items (dates with events, prompts with answers, people with facts) to score points and, more importantly, to generate the real product: conversation. Success isn't measured in retention or streaks — it's measured in "let's play one more round" and "wait, tell me more about that."

## Brand Personality

Warm, playful, charming. The voice is a good host at a dinner party: witty but not sarcastic, encouraging but not patronizing, clever but not trying too hard. The game winks at players without winking at itself. Emotional goal: make people feel at ease, a little delighted, and connected to whoever they're playing with.

## Anti-references

- **Not childish or kid-game**: No primary-color palettes, cartoon mascots, Comic Sans energy, or juvenile sound effects. This is playful for adults — think cocktail-party playful, not playground playful.
- **Not corporate dating-app UX**: No Tinder/Bumble/Hinge aesthetics. No swipes, no glossy headshots, no dating-app tropes. The game is about the people in the room, not the people on the screen.
- **Not gamified SaaS**: No streaks, progress bars, achievement badges, daily challenges, or Duolingo-ification. Play is its own reward.

## Design Principles

1. **Clear at a glance** — Party-game speed. Anyone looking at the screen should understand the state in under a second. No dense layouts, no hidden affordances, no tutorials needed.

2. **Charm without cuteness** — Delightful for adults. Warmth comes from color, motion, and tone — not from mascots, childish illustration, or twee copy. Sophisticated playfulness.

3. **Serve the room, not the screen** — The game is a social catalyst, not the main event. The interface recedes when the conversation starts. The best round is one where people stop looking at the screen and start talking to each other.

4. **Every interaction feels good** — Taps, matches, reveals, and round transitions all have satisfying, tactile feedback. Motion is purposeful and restrained — never gratuitous, never absent.

5. **Accessible by default** — WCAG AA from the start. High contrast, clear type, keyboard-navigable, reduced-motion respectful. A game everyone at the table can play.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Target: WCAG AA compliance
- All interactions must be keyboard-navigable
- Color is never the sole differentiator (match states use shape/icon/text in addition to color)
- Motion respects `prefers-reduced-motion` — animated transitions degrade to instant crossfades
- Type is legible at arm's length (phone-on-table social play)
- Touch targets meet WCAG 2.2 target size (24×24 CSS pixels minimum)
